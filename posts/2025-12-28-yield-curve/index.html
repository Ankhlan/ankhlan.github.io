Yield Curve Inversion: What It Tells Us

The yield curve — the relationship between interest rates and maturities — is one of the most watched indicators in macro. When short-term rates exceed long-term rates (inversion), recessions often follow.

The current picture

The 2-year vs 10-year spread has been a reliable recession predictor:

  • Inverted 1978 → recession 1980
  • Inverted 1989 → recession 1990
  • Inverted 2000 → recession 2001
  • Inverted 2006 → recession 2008
  • Inverted 2022 → ???

Why does it work?

Several theories:

  1. Expectations hypothesis — markets expect the Fed to cut rates due to coming weakness
  2. Risk premium — term premium compression signals flight to safety
  3. Bank lending — flat/inverted curve reduces bank profitability, tightening credit
  4. Self-fulfilling — enough people believe it that it affects behavior

Probably all four contribute.

What to watch

The inversion itself isn't the signal — it's the re-steepening that typically precedes the recession. The curve often normalizes as the Fed begins cutting, which is when the recession arrives.

SpreadLevelSignal
2y-10y+15bpNormalizing
3m-10y-20bpStill inverted
Fed funds - 2y-50bpCuts expected

The lag

Historical lag from inversion to recession: 6–24 months. This time? Unknown.
