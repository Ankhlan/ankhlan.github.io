Dollar Liquidity and the Offshore System

The dollar is unique: it's both a domestic currency and the dominant currency of global trade and finance. This creates a vast "offshore" dollar system that operates largely outside Fed supervision.

The Eurodollar market

Dollars held in banks outside the US — "Eurodollars" — form a multi-trillion dollar market. These aren't physical bills; they're deposits and liabilities denominated in USD but booked abroad.

Key features:

  • No reserve requirements (historically)
  • Outside Fed regulation (mostly)
  • Critical for trade finance and cross-border lending

How offshore dollars are created

When a non-US bank makes a USD loan, it creates a USD deposit — just like domestic banking. The constraint isn't reserves but:

  1. Access to USD funding (wholesale markets, swaps)
  2. Correspondent banking relationships
  3. Regulatory capital

Why it matters

Offshore dollar scarcity shows up as:

  • FX basis blowouts — cost of swapping EUR/JPY to USD spikes
  • Cross-currency basis — premium for USD funding
  • Repo stress — collateral shortages in USD

The Fed has responded to offshore stress with:

  • Swap lines — lending USD to foreign central banks
  • FIMA repo — letting foreign central banks repo Treasuries for USD

The plumbing

Every offshore dollar ultimately traces back to a correspondent account at a US bank. This is the choke point — and why US sanctions are so powerful.
